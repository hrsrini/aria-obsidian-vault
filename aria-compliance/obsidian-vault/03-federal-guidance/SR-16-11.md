## Summary

SR 16-11 is the Federal Reserve's primary supervisory guidance for assessing risk management at institutions with total consolidated assets under $100 billion. It establishes the expectations for board oversight, management accountability, and the three lines of defense model. Examiners use this letter as the primary benchmark for evaluating risk management maturity during examinations.

## Key requirements

- Board must demonstrate active oversight of risk appetite and risk limits — not rubber-stamp approval. See [[ERM-Framework-Requirements]]
- Institutions must maintain documented [[Risk-ID-Assessment-Programs]] covering all material risk categories
- The [[Three-Lines-Defense-Model]] must be clearly delineated: business lines (1st), risk management (2nd), internal audit (3rd)
- CRO must have direct board access and authority independent of business lines. See [[CRO-Obligations-SR16-11]]
- Forward-looking stress scenarios required for credit, market, and liquidity risk
- Risk appetite statement must be board-approved and cascaded to business units

## Supersedes

This guidance supersedes [[SR-95-51]], which applied a narrower governance framework. Key change: SR 16-11 expanded scope to cover all material risk categories under a unified ERM lens and clarified the board's role in setting — not just approving — risk appetite.

## Expert note

Examiners treat SR 16-11 as the baseline scorecard for risk governance maturity at sub-$100Bn institutions. The most frequently cited MRA areas are:

1. Board minutes that show passive approval rather than active challenge
2. Risk appetite statements that lack quantitative limits
3. Blurred lines between 1st and 2nd line responsibilities

See [[Common-Examination-Findings-Governance]] for a full pattern list. The board vs. management distinction is the single most litigated point in examination findings at community and regional banks.
