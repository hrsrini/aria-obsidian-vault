## What this threshold triggers

Crossing $10Bn in total consolidated assets is one of the most consequential regulatory thresholds in US banking. It activates enhanced supervision, new program requirements, and expanded examiner scrutiny across multiple risk domains simultaneously.

## Regulations activated

- [[DFAST-Stress-Testing]] — Annual company-run stress tests required under Dodd-Frank Section 165
- [[CFPB-Supervision-10Bn]] — Direct CFPB supervisory authority activates; OCC/FDIC no longer sole examiner for consumer protection
- [[BSA-AML-Enhanced]] — FinCEN and primary regulator expectations for BSA/AML program sophistication increase materially
- [[SR-12-7-Stress-Testing]] — Federal Reserve stress testing guidance becomes directly applicable
- Enhanced liquidity reporting requirements under [[LCR-Liquidity-Coverage]]

## Program requirements

| Program | Requirement at $10Bn |
|---------|---------------------|
| Stress Testing | Annual DFAST company-run test; results disclosed publicly |
| Consumer Protection | Direct CFPB examination authority |
| BSA/AML | Enhanced transaction monitoring; dedicated BSA officer expectations rise |
| Capital Planning | More rigorous ICAAP expectations |
| Liquidity | LCR monitoring; ILAAP documentation expected |

## Examiner focus at this threshold

Examiners from the Federal Reserve, OCC, and CFPB all coordinate around the $10Bn crossing event. Banks approaching this threshold (typically $8–9Bn assets) should begin gap assessments 18–24 months in advance. Key examiner focus areas:

- Is the stress testing program board-approved and independently reviewed?
- Does BSA/AML technology scale to new transaction volumes?
- Has the institution formally notified CFPB of threshold crossing?
- Are capital buffers adequate under adverse stress scenarios?
